Master's thesis

Potential Drivers of the Record Low Ozone Values in the Arctic Over the Last Three Decades
M.Sc. Dissertation  ·  National Center for Polar and Ocean Research  ·  May – Aug 2023
Dr. Avinash Kumar, Scientist-E — NCPOR, Goa Prof. Shivanna — Dept. of Marine Geology, Mangalore University ERA5 · NetCDF · Python · NCEP/NCAR 60°N – 90°N Arctic polar cap region
Abstract

This study investigates the three most significant Arctic ozone depletion events of the last three decades — March 1997, 2011, and 2020 — using ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis data from ECMWF. In all three cases, abnormally strong and frigid polar vortex conditions produced historically low Arctic ozone. Notably, the minimum ozone column reached an unprecedented low of 224 DU on 18 March 2020, remaining below 250 DU for most of March and the first half of April — an exceedingly rare and severe event. Arctic ozone similarly decreased in 2011 (247 DU) and 1997 (222 DU). ERA5 data revealed minimum polar cap temperatures consistently below 195 K at ~20 km altitude throughout the 2019/2020 Northern Hemisphere winter, facilitating enhanced polar stratospheric cloud formation and sustained ozone loss through heterogeneous chemistry.

Key findings
  • All three extreme ozone depletion events (1997, 2011, 2020) were driven by an exceptionally strong and undisturbed polar vortex throughout the Northern Hemisphere winter and early spring, with no significant sudden stratospheric warming (SSW) events to weaken it.
  • In 2019/2020, minimum polar cap temperatures remained below 195 K at 50 hPa (the NAT-PSC formation threshold) from December to early April — approximately 3.5 months — enabling the longest sustained period of polar stratospheric cloud activity among the three events.
  • The 2020 event produced ozone columns below 250 DU for the entirety of March and the first half of April, with the absolute minimum of 224 DU recorded on 18 March — compared with minimums of 247 DU (2011) and 222 DU (1997, limited duration).
  • Zonal wind analysis at 10 hPa and 50 hPa confirmed strong westerly flow during all low-ozone events, consistent with a robust polar vortex preventing meridional mixing and ozone transport from lower latitudes.
  • The anomalously low base TCO3 within the vortex in 2020, combined with reduced meridional ozone transport and active stratospheric chlorine chemistry (ClO-driven), explains why 2020 surpassed 1997 and 2011 as the most severe Arctic ozone loss event on record.
  • ERA5 data revealed that the winter of 2019/2020 represents an archetypal Northern Hemisphere winter with near-zero planetary wave activity — a pattern that, if more frequent, may signal climate-change-driven stratospheric changes.
Relevance to insurance & catastrophe risk

The analytical methods used in this thesis — large-dataset processing with Python & NetCDF, ERA5 climate reanalysis, spatial risk mapping, anomaly detection, and statistical modelling of extreme events — are directly transferable to catastrophe (CAT) modelling and climate risk roles at firms such as Verisk, Moody's RMS, Aon, and Swiss Re. The study's focus on identifying and quantifying rare extreme atmospheric events mirrors the core work of insurance catastrophe analysts.
